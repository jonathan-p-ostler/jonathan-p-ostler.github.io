Ladies wristwatch. 1956 21st birthday gift. After not being worn for decades I was asked by the original owner to attempt repairs and service the movement. Fiat car clock from the 1920s. Owned by myself. Arrived very dirty and could imagine years of Italian road journeys adding to it's condition. Main issues were very unstable dial paint (a common issue on these) and needing a general clean and parts check.
I like to do just enough to get a watch or clock going again without over restoring the general appearance as the sense of age and it's history can be lost.

Work done:
Stabilised dial paint and sympathetic restoration.
Dismantled, cleaned and checked movement. Lubrication

Movement out of case shows dial in poor state
Sympathetic restoration using aquazol resin to stabilise flaking paint
Grimey movement
Back in it's case and ticking away

Ladies pendant watch circa 1950s-60s. A local customer asked me to attempt repair of their Mother's watch. Of great sentimental value to them, I find making someone happy as a result of getting it going again very gratifying. I was told the main issue was a broken winding stem. On inspection I also found both balance wheel pivots broken, the dial loose and all needed a good clean.

Work done:
Transferred original hairspring onto a spare balance wheel.
Disassembled movement and cleaned parts.
Fitted new winding stem with the original crown.
Improved fit of dial and centre seconds spinner.
lubricated movement
testing

On arrival
Trying the new winding stem before cutting to size and fitting original crown
Needed to improve fit of dial and the centre seconds revolving disc
All done and ready to hand back

Junghans alarm watch circa 1910. Owned by myself. Arrived as non-running. On dismantling I found a broken escape wheel pivot and an oval pivot hole.

Work done:
Re-pivot of escape wheel.
Made and fitted new pivot hole brass bushing. Broached to required diameter.
Movement clean and lubrication.

On arrival
Broken pivot on escape wheel
Repaired
Pivot hole found to be worn to an oval causing the watch to stop
Made and fitted a new bushing to ensure good running


English rack lever pocket watch by P. Litherland. circa 1792. Owned by myself. Running unreliably. On dismantling I found a some teeth on the going barrel had worn.

Work done:
Cut 3 slots in going barrel
Shaped brass pieces to fit slots as new teeth
Soldered new teeth and shaped to size and form

On arrival
Going barrel wear
Showing new brass teeth placed in cut slots
The finished result. The watch now runs reliably
